      <h2><a name="session_355"></a>Bourne Again. Bootstrap a testing framework in BASH</h2>
      <h3>Enjoying learning bash through bootstrapping a test framework in it - a kata</h3>
      <h3>
          <a href="#presenter_1">Rob Westgeest</a>
      </h3>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr><td colspan="2" ><p>In choosing a language for automating small build tasks feels like choosing a problem. For scripting languages like python and ruby you end up learning and polluting your machine with pip, virtualenv, chruby, rubygems and the like. And while bash is always around, it is not powerful enough! Or is it. Let me take you through an hour of bootstrapping a test framework (i.e. Test-Drive a test framework) in bash. You&apos;ll learn some bash and a great way to learn a new language.
</p></td></tr>

    <td class="propertyname">Goal of the session: </td><td>Learn some bash and a great way to learn a new language  </td>

  </tr>

  <tr><td colspan="2" ><p>Have you ever written a test framework yourself? It is not that hard and a great way to learn a language. In this session I will take bash as an example. Bash is all around and often abandoned as a serious automation language and if used certainly not tested well enough and written using a trial and error method.</p><p>In this session i will take you through the steps of bootstrapping a testing framework in baby steps, very much in the style of Kent Beck&apos;s Test Driven Development by example. In other words we&apos;ll Test Drive a bash unit testing framework. </p><p>The session is an exercise in writing your own testing framework as much as it is a learning experience in bash.

      <h2><a name="session_329"></a>Delegation is an Art</h2>
      <h3>Growing Self-Managing Teams</h3>
      <h3>
          <a href="#presenter_351">Jurgen De Smet</a>
          &amp; <a href="#presenter_353">Annelies De Meyere</a>
      </h3>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr><td colspan="2" ><p>How do you manage to empower teams in such a way that it is useful and beneficiary to the organisation? As we support many organisations we have discovered a lot of helpful tools and techniques: RACI, LRC, DACI, Situational Leadership&hellip; and many more. All of them have some truth in them but there is only one that we experienced working 8 out of 10 times instead of occasionally: &ldquo;Delegation Poker&rdquo; in combination with &ldquo;Authority Boards&rdquo;.</p></td></tr>
  <tr>
    <td class="propertyname">Goal of the session: </td><td>Understand how to grow Self-Managing teams  </td>
  </tr>
    <tr>
      <td class="propertyname">Intended audience:</td><td> Leo, Bram, Georges, Vincent, Joke, Ellen, Marieke, Hank</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td class="propertyname">Expected experience:</td><td> No </td>
    </tr>
  <tr>
    <td class="propertyname">Session Type:</td><td> 75 min discovery session </td>
  </tr>
  <tr><td colspan="2" ><p>In this rapidly changing world where change itself has changed, we need more people to be part of the system to provide evolutionary, disruptive and open innovation! Internet connections, data storage, mobile devices, genome sequencing, everything today is growing at an exponential rate where organisations need to be more adaptive, and change as fast as change itself. </p><p>*<b>Top-down controlled, managed organisations are bound to die</b>* as there is no time to aks for permission! The only way to survive in this new world is to get your organisation to engage with their (potential) customers and with their employees in order to find the change it needs to survive within this jungle of opportunities. *<b>Being slow is not an option anymore!</b>* Producing low quality is not an option anymore! Neglecting your customers is not an option anymore!</p><p>One of the options at hand is to *<b>grow self-managing mechanisms within your organisation that are empowered to swiftly act upon the opportunities</b>* at hand. </p><p>But how do you manage to empower teams in such a way that it is useful and beneficiary to the organisation? As we support many organisations we have discovered a lot of helpful tools and techniques: RACI, LRC, DACI, Situational Leadership&hellip; and many more. All of them have some truth in them but there is only one that we experienced working 8 out of 10 times instead of occasionally: &ldquo;Delegation Poker&rdquo; in combination with &ldquo;Authority Boards&rdquo;.</p><p>In this hands-on session we&apos;ll let you discover the power of those 2 techniques yourself and be able to apply them in your day-2-day work (even private) life.</p></td></tr>

  <tr><td colspan="2" ><p>Based on my experience facilitating code retreats I have had many similar questions and challenges over the last few years. One concept that I challenge every single pair on at some time is the single responsibility principle. When developing different concepts of one problem it is very easy to break single responsibility, just because it is convenient to do so.</p><p>During this session we will do a short modelling exercise on a given problem after which we will split the group in pairs.   <tr><td colspan="2" ><p>Listening is the basic skill of connection, learning, and collaboration. 
<br />Listening provides the space for development of ideas, for co-creating. 
<br />Sadly, we rarely listen with mindful attention. We rarely listen with all our senses. We rarely listen to the space, the systems, our software, our world. We rarely listen to what happens within ourselves.
<br />We have been programmed to listen in order to respond.
<br />We want to learn together to listen in order to understand, to sense, to deeply connect with ourselves and each other.
<br />eXtreme Listening will unlock unused dimensions and amplify skills of understanding you never knew you had.</p><p>When - while you are listening - you start thinking about the other person THEN you&rsquo;re not listening. Most of us think about what to say next, what advice to give, how we can relate the other person&apos;s story to our stories - while we&rsquo;re listening, and then we&rsquo;re not really listening. So listening is not only about stop talking. Listening is also about stop thinking, about anything other than the other person and what the other person is telling you.
<br />And it&rsquo;s a whole new experience. Intense, almost therapeutic. For both you as the listener and for the other person being listened to. As the listener you obviously understand much better when you really listen. And it&rsquo;s almost a relief, a certain state of mind: you give yourself a break from your own thoughts and just let yourself become a part of the other person&rsquo;s stories for a while. For the person being listened to, it&rsquo;s amazing - for once - having the full attention, the space for a conversation about you, and only about you. When you get questions from the listener, the questions are only to explore your story, and to create a better understanding of your story. This - funny enough - improves the understanding of both the listener and the person being listened to. What a starting point for leading, coaching, sparring and collaboration of any kind. Pst: Come and listen! And be listened to!

      <h3>Or, how learning from development practices made us better managers...</h3>

  <tr><td colspan="2" ><p>What can people in a lead position learn from the best practices and principles applied in eXtreme Programming? In this session we will share how we apply values from XP in our day-to-day work. How we apply unit testing, apply collective ownership and do &apos;pair programming&apos; in management.</p></td></tr>

  <tr><td colspan="2" ><p>In this session, we will discuss how certain values, best practices and rules found in XP can also be applied by leads. We have found there to be a big overlap between XP and management techniques. You do not need to be a manager to act as a manager.</p><p>For example, we will share how you can apply:
<br /><ul><li>XP values such simplicity, feedback, courage and respect in managing your multi disciplinary, distributed scrum teams. 
<br /></li><li>Best practices such as coding your unit tests first and how to apply that in management. Whenever we apply a change in the organization we think about how we can measure the results of these changes.
<br /></li><li>Use of collective ownership. Delegate and let other people become co-owners of processes and procedures.
<br /></li><li>Integrate early and often. Have frequents one on ones to stay tuned to what is happening in your teams.
<br /></li></ul></p><p>We will give examples how thoughts found in XP have been applied by us as management principles.</p></td></tr>
